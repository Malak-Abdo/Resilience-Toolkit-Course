📖 Scenario 1: "Keith – The Expert referral for CD position but can't make it!"

Keith had 12 years of excellent technical experience in his field. He was highly skilled. Because of his great work, he was recommended for a top position: Country Director in Yemen for an international aid organization (INGO).

During the technical test, Keith did a very good job. He proved he knew the technical side perfectly. But in the final interview, he told the panel:
"The Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs / official rules) we use in our New York office can be used directly in Yemen. I will work with the local team to find a way to use them there."

The interview panel asked him how he would handle local cultural changes and tribal rules on the ground. Keith answered firmly:
"Our existing rules should not be changed. They must only be followed strictly to keep our quality high. I expect the local team to find a way to make them work."

This rigid mindset was one of the main reasons Keith was not selected for the role.

Why was Keith not selected? (Diagnosis)

Keith was rejected not because he lacked technical skill - he excelled at the technical test. Keith was not selected for several reasons, including:

• He ignored local cultural and contextual variables in Yemen, a volatile, high-risk humanitarian setting.
• He assumed that SOPs designed in New York (a stable, resourced, rule-bound environment) were directly transferable without modification.
• He said he would demand the local team "find a way" to apply those SOPs - shifting the entire burden of adaptation onto local staff, without giving them authority, flexibility, or resources.

This approach would lead to staff exhaustion, breakdown of local trust, and total loss of motivation.

Diagnosis: Keith was performance-oriented under stability, but strategically rigid under volatility. Technical skill gets you to the door. Adaptive skill decides if you are allowed to lead through it.
📖 Scenario 2: "Leyla – The Flexible Leader"

Leyla was the Country Director for an international aid organization (INGO) in Sudan, a place with heavy, active conflict.

In the middle of an important, life-saving project, a major fuel shortage suddenly hit the entire region. No fuel was available anywhere. The shortage was expected to last for at least 7 days.

Without fuel, none of the organization's trucks or cars could move. The field teams could not travel to deliver food, open health clinics, or check on communities in need.

All work stopped. The entire team looked at Leyla, waiting for her decision.

If you were in Leyla's position, what would you do?
Do you get angry and blame your logistics team for not keeping extra fuel?
Do you stop all work and wait days for international Headquarters (HQ) to approve buying fuel from alternative local sources?

⚠️ FIELD REALITY CHECK:
Leyla did NOT solve the crisis with a single question. She faced pushback, logistical nightmares, and ethical dilemmas. Adaptability does NOT mean problems disappear. It means you get tasks done in less time, protect your team's morale, and find the "next right action" instead of freezing.

Note: Adaptability does NOT mean breaking rules. It means knowing when the context requires bending the protocol to save lives, and when strict compliance is the only safe path.
💡 Core Takeaway: Always gather local knowledge FIRST! Headquarters needs field data to make safe, fast choices. To avoid delays, never just dump a problem on HQ—always send your local plan alongside your risk assessment to unlock fast approvals.
